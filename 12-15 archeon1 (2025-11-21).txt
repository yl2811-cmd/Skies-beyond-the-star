12.A:Skyborne phoenix

A gentle breeze skimmed Anna's cheek and woke her before the sun had cleared the rim of the world. She'd fallen asleep where she'd meant only to sit—on the airship's wooden deck, boots crossed, jacket pulled up to her chin after a midnight round of checks.

Now the sky was taking color: thin bands of orange and rose combed the cloud tops, and far above, faint green curtains of aurora threaded the blue like quiet seams pulled through cloth.

The turbines murmured under her, a steady, bone-deep hum that meant everything sat where it should. She stood, stretching until her shoulders popped, then crossed to the port nacelle and set her palm flat against the curved housing. The metal was cool with stored night. The vibration was even, a low, contented thrum.

Cold, thin air filled her lungs—sharp as resin and frost. Overhead, the envelope's skin took the first light like brushed steel, every patch and seam picked out in a pale gleam. The ship rode the current easy, a heavy thing made gentle by good trim and a quiet sky.

"Morning," she told the deck, out of habit.

The answer came in small, familiar hints: a slightly brighter whirr from the port boost, the faint click of a cooling brace settling. Nothing out of place. Good morning.

By the forward coil, a hunched shape hadn't noticed the day at all. Milo sat with a battered book open across his knees, pencil laid in the gutter to pin a diagram. The cover had been handled down to a soft sheen; the pages were rippled, as if they'd once gone through a storm and dried in a hurry.

In the corner of the spread, the old Earth printer's globe-stamp was half rubbed away. Milo's fingertip followed a sketch in faded script: twin towers, a spool, a ladder of cable—ideas from a planet that had once thrown steel into orbit and now existed only as ghosts on the page.

Anna padded over and ruffled his hair. "Boo."

He jumped, clapped the book shut, and looked instantly guilty. "I wasn't slacking, I was—"

"At dawn? With a manual older than both of us together?" she said, deadpan. "That's elite-grade slacking." A grin leaked through the act. She nudged the cover with a grease-stained knuckle. "Come on. Show me."

He opened to the spread again. Hand-drawn sheaves and bearings, neat arrows, numbers with their decimal points long gone. He glanced sideways at her, watching for mockery.

"Big ideas," she said instead. "I like big ideas." She tipped her chin toward the real machinery forward. "But we still fall if we forget the little ones. Fuel lines. Clamps."

"I tightened the forward thruster clamps last night," he blurted, quick with pride.

"Good." She crouched beside him and mimed turning a fitting with an invisible wrench, slow and careful. She'd always liked her apprentices proud of the right things. "Little things. That's the part that keeps us from dropping out of the sky."

He repeated the motion in the air, half-turn precise. The book slid; she caught it with a palm. A margin detail hooked her eye: a graceful multi-sheave layout that would take load in smooth steps.

"Neat," she murmured, already slotting it beside the mental list of spare parts in the bin. A better trim-wheel, maybe. Old-world trick, local fix. "Dream big, check small. That's the game."

Milo nodded, eyes bright again.

They walked the length of the deck together. Rigging above ticked and sighed. One pulley gave a quick, high squeal—one note sharp in an otherwise even chord. Anna didn't have to look for it. She stepped to a locker, fished an oiler, reached up, and brushed the pulley's axle with the tip.

The squeal fell to a soft purr, folding back into the ship's usual music.

"Why that one?" Milo asked.

"Only one that sang off-key," she said. "Let your ear sort through the noise and it'll point for you."

The wind pressed colder against her face. Far ahead, the light over the cloud tops was thickening—time to think about descent.

Ahead, the basin skyport stood up out of the fields like a stacked scaffold of timber and containers, a tower punched straight through the low haze. Flags snapped at its crown—long strips of cloth, sun-bleached at the edges, bright at the seams where they'd been mended.

Wind wrote its opinion in them more cleanly than any gauge at this distance. The outer flags strained hard to port; the two nearer the mast hung in the tower's lee, heavy and slow.

Anna pointed. "Shadow line there. Once we're under it, the crosswind drops. Pay attention to when the deck hum changes."

Milo squinted along her hand, mouthing the pattern to himself.

They eased down toward the cloud layer. Sunlight slid higher across the envelope, setting quick sparks along the rivet heads. Cold air threaded through the deck seams, bringing resin and rope tar and a faint, metallic tang from the turbine housings.

Then the layer parted.

They slipped through in a slow slide, and the basin opened underneath: canals laid in iron-green stripes that caught and flattened the sun, fields combed to gold and new-leaf green, windbreak rows casting long, ruler-straight shadows. Off the windward shoulder, the skyport tower rose from the flats, its braces dark with old oil, edges scabbed with rust blooms and fresh weld.

"Two fingers down," Anna said.

She eased the throttle. The turbine note softened; the vibration under her boots dropped half a step. Air thickened against the hull, warm from the ground, smelling of wet soil and the faint smoke of cooking fires carried up in thin threads.

They took the approach wide. Heat shimmered over the flats, warping the fields into bands; the cool draw from the canals undercut it, laying still air in narrow lanes. Anna thumbed a fraction of trim into the rudder. The ship's heavy body eased obediently, nose swinging onto line with the tower.

As they slid into the structure's shadow, the crosswind went out of the hull like breath leaving lungs. Anna felt the change in the soles of her feet before the instruments caught up.

"There," Milo said softly. "Drop."

"Good," she answered. "Remember that timing."

The tower filled the forward view now: ladder runs just wide enough for a cart; brace plates scuffed pale where ropes had worked grooves into them; a bolted beam where the original timber had split and been forgiven instead of replaced. Signal flags on the near face flicked in short, precise motions, returning her approach code.

Lines arced clean from the foredeck, ropes hissing over oak belaying pins. Figures on the platform leaned out to catch them, gloved hands closing with sure economy. Wood boomed under boots in a steady rhythm as they walked the tension into place. A hoist pawl counted teeth—tick-tick-tick—while a deck whistle cut one short note and heads turned up the line in answer.

The outer flags drooped as the hull settled fully into the lee. Vibration in the boards under Anna's boots shifted from the forward-leaning buzz of flight to a lower, steadier pulse.

"Hold her," she murmured, mostly to herself.

The pad rose to meet them. Buffers kissed the hull with a muted, rubbery thud. Clamp arms swung home and seated with a solid, bone-deep knock that shivered through the decking. The envelope vents sighed, releasing a short breath of warm gas across her knuckles as pressure equalized.

The ship's idle settled into a single, even tone.

A dock boss in a sun-faded vest strode up and thumped the hull twice with the side of his fist. The sound came back clean. He lifted his brows. Anna answered with the same two-beat knock. Enough said; he was already turning away, bellowing for the lift crew.

Up here, the platform had its own small weather. Heat bled off sun-struck metal into the air, thick with the smells of wet timber, rope fiber, spilled oil. Ropes creaked in their blocks. Somewhere beyond the tower, a child laughed; a hanging chime struck a single bright note before the wind swung it out of range again.

Anna drew a breath of that dense, lived-in air and let it settle. "Alright," she said. "We're home for the day."

They stepped down from the gangway and the basin skyport opened around them.

Wind snapped at the high banners on the crown of the tower, outer flags pulled stiff and flat, inner ones hanging lazier in the lee. Old ship spars ran as beams overhead, iron straps bright where countless hands had worried the rust down to clean metal. A skybridge three carts wide threw a checkerboard shadow across the planks. Hoists ticked; carts rattled; a loose tarp flapped once in a high, shrill note—

"Chain to the rack!" someone shouted along the concourse.

Anna and Milo slid into place as if the space had been waiting for them. Palms met cool clay; jugs moved hand to hand down a living conveyor. Each shelf took weight with a soft, repeating thud. The line found a rhythm and held it: pass, catch, set, pass.

Two positions down, a sling tore at a cargo hook with a wet canvas rip. The line staggered. Anna dropped into a crouch beside the frayed strap, the smell of dust and hemp thick in her nose.

"Figure-eight, not a granny," she told the teen holding the loose end.

Milo leaned in with the lamp, throwing a tight circle of amber light over their hands. Anna fed the strap through with quick, sure fingers, dressed the knot clean and flat, then set the hand press. One squeeze; the rivet seated with a solid, satisfying bite. She gave the strap a hard tug. It held. The teen nodded, already hauling again, the line's rhythm snapping back into place.

Sun had climbed high enough to squash the shadows short on the planks. A farm rep nosed a cart of produce up to the rail, the wheels leaving dark grooves in the wood where moisture bled from the crates.

"South run at dawn," the woman called out. "Half now, half on delivery."

"Two port, one aft," Anna answered.

She chalked light marks where the lashings would bite, lifted a crate by one corner to taste its weight, then set it on the centerline where the balance wanted it. The quartermaster's seal press came down on the chit with a deep, hollow thump. When Anna thumbed the paper, the raised ring of the basin guild was still warm.

By the port nacelle, two kids stood on tiptoe to trace the painted gear-wing emblem with cautious fingers. Anna flipped a latch and swung a small access panel open, holding it safely away from them.

"Hinge catches here," she said, tapping the stop. "Fingers clear."

The younger child snatched his hand back, eyes wide. Anna shut the panel again, feeling the even vibration through the casing. Mounts tight. No rattle. Good.

Milo had drifted toward a trestle table piled with old manuals and dead slates, their corners grey with thumb-grime. He cracked a scuffed volume at random. Inside, a hand-drawn multi-sheave spool took load in smooth steps, arrows marching around the margin in careful pencil.

"Smarter trim wheel," Anna said, coming up beside him. She tapped the annotation with a knuckle. "We'll try it after lunch."

His fingers tightened around the book's spine, already reverse-engineering the diagram with his eyes.

Overhead, a hoist squealed in protest as it took a fresh pallet. The note cut through the general roar like metal tearing. Anna reached up as it descended past her, dabbing the pawl spring with a smear of grease from her glove. The squeal dropped to a tolerable tick; the lift kept climbing. The crew chief looked down, gave her a quick, grateful flick of two fingers, and turned back to his load.

The pad breathed its work: pawl click, flag snap, cart clatter, rope creak. Warm air pushed from a corridor mouth, a different current layered over the wind—stew steam, scorched chicory, hot metal from a samovar, wool and soap from too many jackets dried too quickly over the same rail. Somewhere, a bell tinkled off-key as a cart bumped a post.

A cook leaned from the doorway, flour dusted up both forearms. "Common house is calling!" she hollered. "You two eating or pretending you don't need it?"

Milo's head turned before Anna's did. His nose had already chosen.

Anna hooked a thumb into her tool belt, feeling the comforting weight of her wrench there. "We're eating," she said, answering for both of them.

They let the current of bodies carry them toward the doors, side by side, the skyport's pulse thrumming up through the planks into their boots.

The heavy, pressure-sealed doors sighed open and admitted them, along with a final swirl of high air that died quickly against the dense warmth inside.

The common house swallowed them whole.

The clean, thin breeze of the gantry gave way to a packed, layered fug—stew steam and damp wool, metal polish, boiled grain, the faint sour note of spilled chicory burned on the heating plate. Voices bounced from beam to beam: laughter, arguments over cargo tallies, the murmur of low, tired talk. Somewhere toward the back, a wind-up music box struggled through the same eight notes, one of them just sharp enough to snag the ear every time it came around.

High overhead, massive structural spars salvaged from some long-dead freighter arched across the ceiling, soot-dark and pitted. Someone had hung small tin charms from a few of them; they chimed erratically whenever the big tower flexed. Long, scarred tables ran in rows below, benches worn to a soft sheen by generations of haulers and farmhands.

Anna steered Milo toward a corner booth tucked under one of the spars. The synth-leather seat was cracked like sun-baked river mud, but the angle gave her a clean view of the main doors and the flow of bodies in and out.

"Hold the spot," she said. "I'll get it."

Milo slid in, book already halfway out of his satchel by the time she turned away.

The serving counter stood along the far wall, wood darkened with age and steam. Anna slapped two heavy brass-toned chits onto it. No scanners, no slates; the cook weighed the metal in her palm, then nodded and reached for the ladle.

Thick stew hit bowls with a wet, comforting sound: root tubers and dark grains, shreds of something that had once walked, all buried under a sheen of grease that promised heat and calories. Two mugs followed, filled with chicory so dark it swallowed the light and sent up a rising, bitter perfume. Anna added a third chit on impulse. The cook's mouth twitched—not quite a smile—as she slid a heel of dense bread onto the tray.

Carrying the load back through the press of bodies demanded the same balance she used on a shifting deck. She set the tray down with a low thud that made Milo's book jump.

"Food," she said.

He tucked a folded ration wrapper between the pages and shut the volume with reluctant care. For a moment, the noise around them faded into the simple sound of spoons against ceramic.

The stew came off the spoon thick and hot, sitting heavy and steady in the stomach. The chicory cut through it like a knife, bitter enough to wake the nerves in the back of the throat.

Around them, the skyport talked. At the next table, a pilot in a sleeveless jacket traced wind patterns across a grease-stained tabletop, complaining about a southern chasm that "kicked like a live thing" last week. Two tables over, a trader held a coil of copper wire up to the light, explaining its purity and provenance to a woman with a highland accent. The woman didn't look at the wire; she watched his face, weighing something entirely different.

Anna let the noise wash through her, catching only what touched tomorrow's route.

When the bowls were mostly empty and the warmth had reached her shoulders, she pushed her dish aside and unrolled a waxed canvas bundle onto the table. The flight chart unfurled with a soft crackle, its surface crowded with faded ink routes, over-written notes, and hand-drawn warning glyphs.

Milo leaned forward, stew forgotten. The canvas smelled faintly of oil and old smoke.

"Alright," Anna said. "Let's talk the line."

Her fingertip traced from a small ink circle near the top edge—this tower's basin—down toward the bottom margin, where the line kinked in a tight hook.

"Here's the wall," she said, tapping the thick, dark band that cut across the map. Her knuckle rasped over the rough patch where someone—her younger hand—had once scratched a lightning bolt. "Orun. Air hits it, drops its rain on this side."

She slid her finger down to a paler strip where the ink had gone thin and the parchment looked tired.

"Downwind of that..." The paper crackled softly.

Milo followed her hand, throat working. "Dry," he said. "All the way to Riverbend."

He watched her finger slide past a narrow, shaded strip just south of the wall. She tapped it once.

"Rain shadow," she said. "This is where the basin's green dies. After that, the Ochre. We don't want to spend extra hours over that if we can help it."

On the chart, a thin red route line hugged close to the printed wall instead of wandering out over open desert. Small symbols clustered along it: spirals, jagged teeth, little circles half-shaded.

"We ride the updrafts here," Anna went on. "They're rough, but they save us fuel. We keep one shelf marked for an emergency set-down—" she touched a grease-penciled valley—"and we don't get clever. Straight in, straight out."

Her finger curled along the hook of the river icon at the bottom, where the red line ended in a neat dot tucked into the bend.

"Riverbend," she said. "If the wind behaves and we launch on time, we hit the canyon mouth just before second moonrise."

Milo's gaze flicked between the abstract lines on the chart and the real food stains, thumbprints, and penciled corrections that turned it into something lived-in.

"And if the wind doesn't behave?" he asked.

Anna snorted softly. "Then we work harder."

She let the moment sit, then rolled the chart back up in one practiced movement. The canvas snapped into a tight cylinder.

"Four-oh-zero wake-up," she said, tucking the chart away. "Deck by four-thirty. We'll have the wall in front of us by first light. Get what sleep you can."

Milo made a face, then nodded. The edges of his fatigue showed now that the heat of the stew had taken the sharpest bite out of his nerves.

Around them, the common house continued its steady churn: bowls clattering into wash basins, a chair scraping back, somebody cursing as hot chicory sloshed onto a hand. The music box clicked to a stop, wound down mid-note, leaving a brief, unfamiliar gap in the noise before conversation flowed over it.

Anna drained the last of her drink, the chicory leaving a bitter, grounding residue on her tongue. Tomorrow would be hard air and hard choices. Tonight was tables and charts and a roof that didn't move.

"Come on," she said, sliding out of the booth, boots squeaking on the sticky floor. "Supplies to pull, lines to check, bunks to find."

Milo grabbed his book and followed. As they stepped back toward the doors, a gust from the tower's open throat pushed against them, carrying in a thin thread of cold from the higher air.

The line south waited beyond it, already drawing itself in her head as clearly as ink on canvas.

When they stepped back out of the common house, the air changed.

Inside had been steam and stew and the soft clatter of spoons. Outside, the main platform hit like a gust: sun-baked metal, tar, rope resin, and a wind that had picked up the heat of a hundred hulls. The tower's spine hummed, a deep vibration that came up through the grating into Anna's boots.

Traffic on the basin skyport had thickened. Cargo slings creaked on overhead rails. A fresh hauler eased in on the far side of the tower, its envelope still dripping cold as deckhands jogged to catch its lines. Somewhere below, a hammer found a steady rhythm against steel.

Anna shifted the weight of the supply chit in her hand, then passed it to Milo.

"Quartermaster's list is split," she said. "I'll take the Guild window—fuel, coolant, wraps. You go down to the market deck. Filters, plugs, that size of clamp." She tapped the etched codes on the chit with a grease-stained thumb. "And exactly this grade of tubing. No substitutions."

He took it with both hands, eyes quick over the marks. "Got it."

"And, Milo." She angled her head, catching his gaze. "No book stalls."

A blush climbed from his collar, but the grin was automatic. "I know the way," he said, already turning toward the central stairwell.

"I'm counting on it," Anna replied.

They split at the junction. She took the narrow set of steps that hugged the inner mast, climbing into the wind shadow of the tower's central housing. Milo disappeared downward, swallowed by the clatter and hum.

The lower market deck ran along the tower's waist, a ring of patched plating and bolted beams that vibrated with every shift of the upper moorings. Here the neat Guild signage gave way to hand-painted boards and faded canvas. Stalls leaned into one another, improvised from old hull panels and shipping crates, each one spilling a different smell into the air: hot oil, dust, frying batter, the sharp tang of solvent.

Milo threaded through the crush with the practiced sidestep of someone who had grown up around moving cargo. A woman with sun-burned forearms swung a crate of dried roots past his shoulder; he slipped behind her, followed the line of blue chalk arrows toward the hardware stalls.

He slowed at a break in the row. At one blanket-covered patch of deck, a prospector in a faded flight jacket crouched by a split geode, its interior all violet glass and delicate teeth. Across from him, a farmer in a mud-stained apron guarded baskets of knotted orange tubers. Their hands did the talking.

The prospector rolled the stone once, letting the light catch it, then nudged it closer. The farmer shook her head, tapped the larger basket with two fingers, then the smaller. The prospector considered, jaw working. After a beat, he tilted the geode, breaking off a thinner, still-glittering wedge with a well-placed twist against the deckplate.

He set the shard beside the smaller basket.

The farmer's mouth twitched. She nodded once. They traded—her tubers vanishing into his net bag, his fragment into her callused hand. Not a word.

Milo made himself move again. It would be too easy to stop and watch everything.

The hardware stalls appeared in a muddled cluster: coils of hose, bins of couplings, faded labels peeling at the edges. He found the one he wanted by the look of the stock, not the sign—a bench with every clamp sorted in proper size, each bin's contents aligned like someone had cared.

"Quarter-inch, heat-rated, twelve count," he said, sliding the chit onto the counter.

The stall-keeper, a narrow man with magnifier lenses flipped up on his forehead, scanned the marks, then dove into the bins with quick, efficient hands. Tubing, filters, clamps, each piece thunked onto the counter in order. Milo checked them against the list, thumb lingering a moment on the printing of the pressure rating before he nodded.

As the man boxed the order, something in the back of the stall caught Milo's eye: a small rack of precision work glinting against a rag of dark cloth. Tiny brass and copper coils, each wound so tightly and evenly that the turns looked like they had been cut, not bent. No two were quite the same diameter.

Milo's fingers twitched.

He paid for the Guild order first, stowed the wrapped package into his canvas bag. Only then did he ask, a little too casually, "Those coils—how much for the small one?"

The stall-keeper glanced over his shoulder. "Those?" He picked one up between thumb and forefinger, weighing it. "Hand-wound. Won't make you any money back." His gaze flicked to Milo's worn jacket, then to the chit. "Half a credit."

Milo hesitated just long enough to be sure he wasn't being played, then dug a thin, scuffed token from his pocket. It left his fingers with a tiny reluctance. The coil dropped into his palm, unexpectedly heavy, its wire lattice smooth and cool.

It was nothing they needed for this run. It was also perfect.

He slipped it into his inner pocket, fabric pressing close over the little knot of metal, then hitched the bag higher on his shoulder and turned back toward the stairs. Somewhere above, a whistle shrilled three notes—the mooring crews changing shift. Time getting on.

Up on the Guild tier, things were cleaner and duller.

Anna stepped up to the quartermaster's counter—reinforced composite, no decoration, a low lip worn silky by years of elbows. Behind the thick pane, a bored clerk scanned her Guild ID, the terminal casting his face in a flat greenish light. Beyond him, shelves ran back into shadow, each slot labeled, each container identical.

"Fuel ration B-seven, coolant B-class, wrap, grade four insulation, allotment per schedule," she said, reciting the requirements before he asked.

He didn't look up. "Confirmed." A faint clack of keys. "You're cleared for one drum of fuel, two coolant, four wraps. Extra insulation is discretionary."

"The Plateau threw ice on us last season," Anna said. "Channel route again. You want the report, I can file it, but I'd rather file a safe return."

That earned her the barest lift of his eyebrows. Another pause in the key clicks. "I can release two extra rolls of wrap," he said finally. "Marking as weather compensation. Bring back what you don't use."

"We always do."

Lockers slid open with the soft sigh of pressure seals, canisters and grey-wrapped bundles emerging on the belt. Anna checked the serial tags as she loaded them onto a handcart—numbers, weight, the faint slosh in the drums as the cart rolled away. Everything in its place. No romance at all. She found comfort in that.

By the time she pushed the cart back onto the mooring deck, the sun had rolled a handspan higher. Heat shimmered above the plating, the air a little thinner here at the tower's outer skin. Milo was already waiting by the gangway, bag at his feet, cheeks flushed from the climb.

He straightened when he saw the cart. "Got everything?"

"Most of a tower," Anna said. "You?"

He nudged his bag with his boot. Metal clinked faintly. "Filters, clamps, tubing. The right kind." A brief, guilty pause. "And... one small extra."

Her look lingered for a beat on his face, then slid to the bag. "As long as all the right things are in there, we'll argue about treasures when we're not burning daylight."

That earned a quick grin of relief. Together they wrestled the drums up the ramp, the cart's wheels thudding over the seams in the decking. The Guild Hauler's hull loomed ahead, envelope shadow cutting a clean line across the tower's sunlit skin.

They broke the work into a familiar sequence.

On the forward deck, in the strip of space between the main hatch and the rail, Anna chalked a neat column on the battered route board bolted to the bulkhead. The wood held a ghost of past runs—half-scrubbed names and faint lines where tape had once laid.

"Fuel, coolant, wrap, dry rations," she said, tapping each column as Milo dumped the contents of his bag and began to sort. "One stack for the cold belt, one for the Channel, one for what Riverbend actually pays us for."

He handed her a bundle of insulation. The wrap crackled in her hands, metallic fibers catching the light. She marked a small X beside the "Channel" column and leaned the roll against the port rail. "That one goes on the forward coolant lines. We'll hit the worst of the crosswinds there."

"Got it." Milo stacked clamps in groups of three, his brow furrowed as he matched sizes. Every time a set landed where it should, he dragged the chalk across the board, a small sharp line that felt like progress.

The drums of fuel were last. They muscled them toward the hatch, heels digging in against the deck's faint give. The metal sides were cool now; by afternoon they would be hot enough to sting. Anna knocked each once with the side of her boot, listening to the depth of the note, then chalked their numbers into the "core" column. Fuel wasn't romantic either. It was distance, and distance was margin.

When the piles on the deck matched the marks on the board, she wiped the chalk dust from her fingers onto her trousers and reached into the little tin screwed low on the bulkhead. Paper edges rasped against her skin.

The route slips were cut from old manifests, backs printed with ghost-rows of figures. The fronts carried careful handwriting. She thumbed past last week's loops—north run, ridge farms, a ferry to the east canyons—until she found the one she wanted.

"Riverbend – south run," the slip read in neat, tight script.

She peeled the backing, leaned close, and smoothed it onto the board in the empty space under the week's dates. For a second she pressed her thumb on the center, pushing out the tiny air bubble that wanted to live there. Ink, chalk, a bit of glue: tomorrow, wrapped.

Milo watched, arms full of coiled hose. "Feels real now," he said quietly.

"It was real when we signed the charter," Anna answered, stepping back to look at the board, already thinking three steps ahead. "This just gives the tower something to talk about."

A gust caught the edge of the paper and tried to lift it. The glue held. Above them, a flag clapped hard against its line and then went slack again, settling into a restless flutter. The board vibrated under Anna's hand with the hum of the ship, alive and waiting.

By the time the tower lights had turned from white to a softer amber, the basin had changed again.

The heat had gone out of the metal. Shadows pooled under the platforms and around the feet of mooring pylons. One by one, stall lanterns guttered and went dark. Only the work lights stayed—hard points of white along the tower's spine and a single, steady glow at the tip, marking the skyport's presence to whatever crossed this slice of air after midnight.

On the Hauler's deck, the noise had settled to a low murmur. Distant laughter rose once from the common house, muffled by bulkheads and distance, then faded. The ship's own voice—turbines on idle, auxiliaries cycling in a slow, patient rhythm—filled the space where the day's clatter had been.

Anna stood with her head and shoulders inside the open engine cowling, sleeve pushed up to the elbow. The air in the bay was warmer than the deck, carrying the dense, oily scent of coolant and hot metal slowly surrendering its heat.

"Hand me the last wrap," she said.

Milo, perched on a crate just outside the bay, passed the roll in without looking away from the pressure gauge he had been staring at for the last thirty seconds. The needle, steady in the middle of its arc, had held him as surely as any story.

Anna wound the insulation around a section of exposed line, fingers moving with the easy precision of repetition. The metallic fabric crackled softly as it settled, clamps clicking shut under her wrench.

"There," she said, tightening the last one by feel. "That line's not freezing solid on us this time."

Milo hesitated, then asked, "Is this the line that almost went last season?"

"No." She bumped the cowling up a notch and pointed with the wrench toward a squat, scarred pump bolted low on the frame. "That one is."

He slid off the crate and leaned in. The pump looked wrong, even to his young eye—its casting a shade darker, the mounting bracket clearly adapted from something older. The bolts that held it weren't quite uniform; one had a bright new head, the others were worn smooth.

"It doesn't match," he said.

"It's not from here." Anna ducked out of the bay and wiped her wrist across her forehead, leaving a dark streak. "Came off a prospector's skif. They'd broken half their cooling lattice on a bad landing and limped three cycles on that pump and a prayer."

She touched the casing with the back of her fingers, as if greeting an old acquaintance. "It kept their core from seizing long enough for me to tow them out. It's ugly, and it pulls a little more power than it should, but it runs when things go wrong." A short, firm nod. "It's earned its place."

Milo studied the pump with a new respect. The mismatched bolts stopped being sloppy; they became scars.

He glanced down at his own pocket, feeling the outline of the tiny copper coil through the fabric. Bits of other lives, carried forward.

"Close her up," Anna said. "Gently."

He swung the cowling down. The latch settled with a solid, satisfying clunk. The deck beneath them thrummed as a generator somewhere deep in the hull cycled, smoothing into a low, stable note.

Anna peeled off her right glove.

The air against her bare skin was cooler now, the sweat on her knuckles making the night wind feel sharp as she stepped up to the engine housing. Without ceremony, she laid her palm flat against the curved metal of the nacelle.

For a moment, she simply stood like that, eyes half-closed. The hum came up through the casing and into her hand: not quite sound, not quite motion, a blend of both. Her fingertips rested along a line of rivets. Under the faint vibration she could feel the tiny, regular pulses of the auxiliary pumps, the occasional stutter as a valve shifted somewhere below.

Her fingers moved, slow and methodical, tracing the seam from one mounting bracket to the next. At the junction, she bent her hand and tapped once with her knuckles.

The answer was dull and even. No hollow ring, no bright, worrying ping.

She exhaled.

Faces turned up to her as she emerged—sun-browned, canyon-pale, all sorts. Eyes squinted against the hauler's dimming nav lights. A child perched on a crate clutched a dented tin cup in both hands, knuckles white.

A woman in a grease-stained harness shouldered through the little knot of people and took the ramp in three long strides.

Anna recognized her from the guild briefs: Sila, Riverbend's intake master. Up close, she looked carved from the same cliff as the settlement—ropey muscle, hair bound in a tight knot, canyon wind having left fine lines at the corners of her eyes.

"You cut that landing fine," Sila said without preamble. "But I'll take fine over late."

Anna didn't bother defending herself. "Cargo's secured. You got the rigging for the primaries?"

Sila jerked her chin at the pad's edge. Two overhead hoists waited there, chains already rattling as riverfolk hauled them into position. The metal frames creaked and a cable sang as it took the empty strain.

"We rigged all morning," Sila said. "The intake's choking on silt. If we don't swap these in today, we start rationing by tomorrow."

The words hit Anna harder than the last gust in the Channel.

Rationing tomorrow. That meant taps still ran now—thin, maybe cloudy, but not yet dry. It meant families standing at their filters tonight wondering if someone would arrive in time.

Behind Sila, the child with the cup licked cracked lips and glanced toward a spigot bolted to a nearby pipe, where only a slow, stubborn drip emerged.

"We've got them," Anna said. "Milo?"

He was already at the cargo bay controls, fingers flying over the panel. The hauler shuddered as locks cycled; deep inside, heavy masses shifted with a dull, underwater thump.

One by one, the overhead doors along the hauler's flank irised open. The canyon took a breath.

The first crate emerged on the built-in rail, a block of reinforced metal and composite marked with the guild's stamped sigils and tiny etched numbers. Anna's gaze snagged on them—serial strings she remembered from the Quartermaster's ledger, the ones Milo had argued for until he'd gone hoarse.

"Filtration primaries," he called, almost unnecessarily, as the crate reached the ramp edge.

Chains snapped tight. The hoist took the weight with a shriek of metal on metal. For a moment the crate hung between ship and settlement, swaying over open air as the canyon wind pawed at it.

Then it swung inward, toward Riverbend.

Applause cracked out from the gathered crowd—small at first, just a few pairs of hands, then more as others took it up. Not a festival roar, not yet. It sounded like something caught between relief and stubbornness, like people clapping at the sight of their own survival made tangible and heavy and real.

Anna watched the crate swing away toward the tower housing, watched Sila move with it, shouting orders as workers ran ahead to clear the bolting points. She let some of the tightness drain from her shoulders.

We made it before tomorrow, she thought. Before rationing.

Now all they had to do was keep the canyon, and whatever waited in it, from taking that margin back.

The first crate vanished into the canyon on the hoist, then the next.

Anna and Milo followed along a narrow service catwalk bolted to the cliff, the deck grating flexing faintly under their weight. Below, the Ishkar muttered over stone; above, the hauler crouched on its pad like an oversized insect, turbines still ticking as they cooled. Between the two hung Riverbend's intake tower.

Up close, the structure looked like something that had grown there by accident. Pipes of different ages and alloys crisscrossed a vertical frame of welded scrap. Old paint peeled in phantom bands. Someone had wrapped fluttering strips of cloth around the main riser—faded blues and greens, snapping in the canyon wind.

Workers swarmed over the tower housing, clipped in by harness lines. The old primary was already half-unbolted, its casing filmed with a dull, rust-brown sheen. One of the crew slapped the metal with a gloved hand, and a trickle of muddy water leaked from a hairline crack.

"Kill the feed," a voice shouted. A valve spun with a complaining groan. The drip slowed, then stopped.

The first new unit swung in on the hoist, chains clattering, its fresh markings sharp against the battered frame. Anna watched as Sila and two others guided it into place with practiced pushes and curses, aligning ports with waiting flanges.

"Higher—hold—there," Sila called. "Clamp it."

Bolts tightened with the deep, toothy grind of metal meeting metal. One by one, the clamps locked home. The new primary settled into the tower like a heart into a chest.

"Feed," Sila ordered.

Somewhere above, another valve turned. The tower shuddered. For a moment nothing happened—only the creak of stressed metal and the faint tick of cooling welds. Then a low, throaty hum rolled through the frame, into the catwalk under Anna's boots, up through her bones.

Water hit the housing with a muffled thump.

A sight glass set into the pipe just below the primary clouded brown, then almost black as weeks of accumulated grit shifted. Milo leaned forward, squinting. The murk swirled, thick as smoke. Anna felt her jaw clench.

Come on, she thought, absurdly, as if the water could hear her.

The flow steadied. The darkness inside the glass began to thin, slow streaks of clearer liquid pushing through the sludge. Strata formed: a heavy layer of silt at the bottom, a narrower band above like weak tea, and at the top—

There. A rising lens of almost colorless water, catching the canyon's thin light.

Milo let out a breath he'd been holding so long his shoulders sagged with it. "She's clearing."

The first tap they reopened was bolted to a branch line just off the tower, no more than a rusted spout over a battered catch-basin. A small knot of people had gathered there—two adults in stained work harnesses, an elder with a cane, the child Anna had seen before with the tin cup.

"Hold up," one of the adults said quietly as the child surged forward. "Let her run."

They opened the tap.

What came out first was a thick, brown burp that stained the basin like spilled ink. It spattered the stone and splashed the child's bare feet. He didn't flinch, just watched the flow with fierce concentration.

The color shifted as the first flush passed. The stream thinned, lightened, yellow-brown, then pale straw, then—

Clear.

The water that poured out now was glassy, catching a ghost of reflected rock and sky. The elder dipped two fingers into it, rubbed thumb against fingertip, then nodded once.

"Cups," she said.

The child thrust his tin cup under the stream. It filled with a ringing, uneven patter. He turned, hands trembling with the effort of not spilling, and presented it to the elder first.

She took a careful sip. Her eyes closed. When she opened them, she glanced up at Anna standing on the catwalk above and lifted the cup a fraction in a silent salute before handing it back.

"Go on," she murmured. "You waited long enough."

The boy drank with both hands, eyes closing in sheer, uncomplicated relief. A thin line of water escaped at the corner of his mouth and ran down his chin. He caught the last drops with his tongue.

Around the tower, other taps were coming alive—the cough and rattle of disused valves, the rising scatter-pattern of streams hitting basin metal, the first laughter when someone's cupped palm overflowed. A different kind of noise than the canyon's usual—lighter, higher, edged with disbelief.

Anna swallowed against the tightness in her throat; this, she thought, not the landing, not the paperwork, was the real contact. This was the moment the Guild's lines on a map became something that sat in a child's hands.

She found Sila's gaze through the tangle of pipes and people. The intake master gave a short nod, no frills.

"We'll get the second unit seated before full dark," Sila said. "You did your part. Come on. Lower walk'll want to see who they're raising cups to."

The path down from the tower wasn't a path so much as a suggestion.

It began as a straightforward ramp bolted to the cliff face, then kinked sideways around a jut of rock where some past slide had torn away the original supports. From there, it dissolved into a sequence of switchbacks: wooden staircases with mismatched steps, metal grates grafted onto old beams, narrow landings that doubled as people's front stoops.

Her boots clanged softly on each tread, the sound swallowed almost at once by the canyon's depth. She kept one hand on the rail—not because she didn't trust her balance, but because the drop was always there at the edge of her vision, a vertical sweep of rock and metal and moving figures.

Milo followed a step behind, his head turning constantly as he tried to take everything in.

A half-open doorway exhaled a burst of warm, spiced air—tubers frying in oil, garlic and something sharp. Children's voices bubbled from inside, the rise and fall of a story being told and interrupted and retold with new embellishments.

The air changed as they descended. The chill that still clung to their flight suits loosened its grip, replaced by a layered warmth: sun trapped by stone, bodies packed into tight spaces, cookfires breathing against rock. The damp mineral scent of the Ishkar grew stronger, undercutting the smoke.

Below them, the terraces she'd glimpsed from the air revealed more of themselves.

From this angle, she could see the water channels that fed them—narrow stone-lined troughs carrying thin streams out from the tower. The newly cleared flow already gleamed brighter in them, refracting bits of sky. People were out on the ledges, checking sluice gates, lifting small barriers to let more water spill into plots where young rice waited, knee-high and incredibly green against the canyon's stone.

Anna paused on a landing to watch a man reach down and scoop water from a channel in both hands, letting it pour over his wrists before flicking his fingers toward the river below in some gesture too practiced to be idle.

"Everything here hangs on that tower," Milo said quietly.

"Everything out where we came from does, too," Anna replied. "You just don't see it from this close."

They moved on.

At one bend, the cliff had been cut back to make space for a common platform. A panel of thick transparency had been set into the outer wall there, salvaged from some older structure. Its surface was clouded with fine scratches, but enough clarity remained that the canyon beyond showed through as a warped, shimmering picture.

A cluster of kids had claimed the spot. They pressed their faces and hands to the cool material, laughing at the way it flattened their noses and smeared their reflections into streaks of color. As Anna passed, one of them turned and gave her a solemn thumbs-up, as if authorizing her presence in their vertical world.

She found herself smiling back before she could stop it.

Step by step, the adrenaline of the flight and the sharp, focused edge of the landing bled out of her muscles, replaced by a slower, heavier fatigue. The steady jolt of each footfall on metal became almost hypnotic. The murmur of voices, the creak of boards, the distant rush of the river knitted together into a single, continuous sound that wrapped around her like cloth. Somewhere below, a low, irregular tapping had started to work its way up through the metal, more felt than heard.

For a few levels, no one tried to talk to them. People acknowledged them with nods, or with that quick, assessing glance locals gave any newcomer, then went back to coiling line, to stirring pots, to checking the way light fell on their small balconies of soil.

The settlement wasn't a backdrop; it was busy staying alive. The Guild liked to reduce everything to routes and margins and tonnage, but every bolt in the metal under her boots argued with that math.

They heard the drums before they saw the gathering.

At first it was only that faint, irregular thump woven into the background noise of the canyon, indistinguishable from the clang of hammers and the rattle of carts on grating. But as they descended, it separated itself, found a rhythm.

Boom. Boom-boom. Pause. Boom.

Anna felt it through the soles of her boots more than in her ears, a small tremor each time they crossed a particular seam in the metal. The air on the lower levels carried a new scent, too—woodsmoke sharper and less oily than the cooking fires above, resinous and clean.

"Is that... music?" Milo asked.

"Rhythm," Anna said. "Music comes after someone decides it means something."

He opened his mouth to ask more, then closed it again as they rounded another bend.

The ramp spilled them out onto a broader balcony carved directly into the cliff, its outer edge protected by a railing of welded pipe and mesh. From here, the canyon dropped away in a clean plunge. Below, a wide ledge jutted out over the river—a natural shelf that had been shaped and smoothed and reinforced into a kind of plaza.

People were gathering there.

From this height, they were mostly movement and color. Lanterns hung from cables strung between pylons, small pale moons that swung gently in the air currents. Their glow turned faces into warm ovals, caught in fragments as the crowd shifted.

At the center of the ledge rose a stacked cone of wood and scrap, a fire layed but not yet fully committed. Thin tongues of flame licked at the lowest branches, shedding curls of smoke that spiraled upward to dissipate against the cliff.

The drums were set off to one side—barrels, mostly, with stretched membrane heads. A handful of people stood around them, hands moving in coordinated patterns that sent vibrations up through the rock.

"Looks like they were planning a gathering either way," Milo said under his breath.

Anna leaned her forearms on the railing, the cool pipe steady under her palms. From here she could see the river directly below the ledge, its surface dark and slick where the canyon shadow swallowed most of the sky. Here and there, ripples caught lanternlight and shattered it into coins that danced downstream.

She imagined how it would look when the fire below truly caught, when its reflection burned in that water. Sky Fire on the Ishkar, in miniature.

A figure stepped out onto the edge of the ledge, silhouetted against the unlit bulk of the pyre. Their voice carried up the cliff, thin but clear between drumbeats—formal words Anna couldn't quite catch from this height.

Below, someone touched a torch to the base of the stacked wood. Flames flared, then settled to a low, licking glow.

Anna pushed away from the railing.

"Come on," she said. "If we're going to stand in this, we might as well stand where we can hear what they're actually saying."

They started down the last run of stairs toward the ledge, the drumbeat rising to meet them.









15.A:The Gathering Fire 

By the time Anna and Milo reached the lower levels, the canyon was mostly shadow.

The last of the daylight had drained out of the seam of sky overhead, leaving a narrow strip of deep blue cut between black walls of stone. Lanterns had taken over the work of sight. They swung from cables and brackets, their pale halos smearing across metal and rock, turning every bolt and railing into a point that caught and released light as people moved past.

The stairs ended on a broad ledge of carved stone and plate right at the edge of the Ishkar.

Anna stepped off the last metal riser and onto rock. The change under her boots was subtle but unmistakable—less give, more solid weight. The air down here was cooler than she'd expected, damp and steady, the river's breath washing the dryness of the upper levels from her throat.

A simple rail of welded pipe and mesh ran along the ledge, no higher than her waist. Beyond it the river slid by, dark and slow. It whispered, a constant, low sound like fabric being pulled gently through someone's hands.

Drums thumped somewhere upstream and above, out of sight around a bend in the cliff. The rhythm came in through the stone rather than the air, a pulse Anna could feel in the arches of her feet each time she shifted her weight.

A handful of people were already gathered along the rail. Some sat on crates or flat stones; others leaned on the pipe, elbows hooked, cups dangling from finger-crooks. A few children had claimed spots right at the edge, where they could look down and see the reflection of lanterns running like smudged stars along the river's surface.

Sila had peeled off three landings back, muttering about checking the tower's output one more time. That suited Anna fine. Up here, Sila felt like part of the infrastructure. Down here, Anna wanted fewer moving parts.

"Good line of sight," she said, more to herself than to Milo, as she picked her way to a gap at the rail.

"Line of sight to what?" Milo asked, though he followed her automatically, nudging around a cluster of teenagers passing a jug between them.

"Whatever they're planning to light on fire," Anna said. "And whatever they decide to throw in after it."

He huffed out a nervous laugh. "Comforting."

She rested her forearms on the pipe, cool metal seeping into her skin through the fabric of her jacket. From here, she could just see the lip of another ledge further along the canyon wall, where more lanterns clustered and shadows moved in tighter formation. Drums thickened the air in that direction, beat nested inside beat, like multiple hearts trying to sync.

Whatever the ritual was, it would happen there.

For now, down here was quiet enough that Anna could hear the tick of cooling metal from someone's moored skiff under the ledge, the slosh of water against hull, the small scrape of a cup set carefully on stone.

She let her shoulders drop a little, the day's strain sliding off one notch at a time.

A woman with a wide, tired smile and a coil of hose over one shoulder came along the ledge a few minutes later, moving from knot to knot of people. A portable filter unit rode on a handcart behind her, its pump humming softly, a faint vibration coming up through the stone as she walked.

"Fresh pull," she announced to each cluster. "Tower's running clean. Fill up if you've got something that holds water."

She reached Anna's section of rail and stopped.

Up close, the woman smelled of metal shavings and soap. Her hands were nicked and stained along the fingers, the marks of someone who spent most of her days taking things apart and putting them back together before anyone noticed they'd broken.

"You're the flyer," she said to Anna. It wasn't quite a question.

"Anna," Anna confirmed. "This is Milo."

The woman's gaze flicked over Milo, taking in his too-thin wrists and the grease still smudged at his temple from the tower work. Then she nodded once and thumped the filter unit with the side of her fist.

"This little beast is only being polite because of the work you two brought us," she said. "Cup?"

Anna glanced down. Her own hands were empty.

Before she could answer, someone to her left shoved a tin mug at her. It was the same child from the tower, she realized—the one who'd waited for the first clear stream. The cup looked as if it had been scrubbed within an inch of its metal life, bright patches showing through old dents.

"You can use mine," he said, solemn. "It pours fine."

"Generous," Anna said. "You sure you want to share?"

He nodded, jaw set. "You got the boxes here."

The words landed with more weight than his small voice should have been able to carry.

Anna accepted the cup, feeling the faint warp in the handle where it must have been crushed and bent back sometime in its history. The woman tipped the hose and filled it in one smooth motion. The water came out clear, a thin stream that caught lanternlight and held it for a second before losing it again.

Anna took a sip.

It tasted of stone and metal and faint residual smoke. Underneath all of that, though, there was a clean edge she recognized from every properly-maintained system she'd ever worked with—a rightness in how it sat on her tongue, the way it didn't cling after she swallowed.

"Tower's humming," she said. "Good install."

The woman's smile went smaller but more real. "We do what we can on this side," she said. "You did what you had to on yours."

Milo accepted the cup next, nearly sloshing it in his effort not to look greedy. A couple of people nearby watched him with a mix of curiosity and something like pride, as if seeing one of their older children recite a lesson correctly.

"Feels different than yesterday," someone down the rail murmured after their own first swallow. "Doesn't taste like chewing a nail."

"Filters are seated right," the woman said. "No more ration talk tonight. If they try it, they'll have to fight me and the tower for it."

That drew a low ripple of laughter.

Anna found herself smiling into the rim of the cup despite the ache still threaded through her shoulders. Praise made her uncomfortable; joking made it tolerable.

"You can still ration," she said. "Just ration how many compliments you hand out. Techs get soft if you feed them too much."

Milo made an indignant noise that was swallowed up as someone clapped him lightly on the back.

The boy took his cup back from Anna with exaggerated care. He held it up for a moment, the way the elder had earlier, as if checking that the water's clarity hadn't been some trick of light. Only when he was satisfied did he lift it to his own mouth.

The woman with the hose moved on. The soft hum of her cart faded into the background, replaced again by drums and the river's whisper.

Along the ledge, people raised cups in small, private toasts—to each other, to the tower, to whoever had gotten them through the day. Some glanced up at the strip of sky. Most looked toward the cluster of lanterns around the ritual ledge.

Anna didn't raise hers. She had already drained it. But she rested her knuckles on the rail and let the cool dampness from where a splash had caught the pipe soak into her skin, as close to a gesture as she needed.

The first real flare of fire came just as Anna was starting to lose track of individual drumbeats.

Upstream, on the ritual ledge, a brighter blossom of light surged against the dark. For a heartbeat it was only a glow behind the line of people gathered there. Then it leapt higher, tongues of flame licking up around the stacked wood.

From the riverwalk, the bonfire was framed by the canyon walls like a distant, shifting sun.

Light poured down the rock in streaks, catching on protruding supports and cable anchors. It struck the Ishkar's surface and shattered, scattering in long, broken bands that ran toward Anna and Milo and the others along the rail.

The drums shifted with it.

The loose, layered patterns snapped into a more deliberate shape—four slow beats, then two quicker ones, then a pause. Stone carried the rhythm; the ledge under Anna's boots became a drumhead, transmitting each impact up through her bones.

On the ritual ledge, a voice rose.

Anna couldn't make out the words at first. The sound washed across the canyon like another kind of echo, distorted by distance and rock. Then the voice found a pocket of air that carried better, and the phrase sharpened.

"Tonight we remember," the caller said.

The crowd on the ledge answered, a lower, rougher chorus: "We remember."

The call went out again, the response rolling after it. The words bounced off the canyon walls and slid along the river, dulling slightly with each reflection until they arrived where Anna stood as something more felt than heard.

Beside her, someone on the rail repeated the phrase under their breath, syncing their own cadence to the drums. Further along, a pair of teens joined in with louder voices, one of them slightly off the beat and earning an elbow to the ribs for it.

Milo craned his neck to see better, standing on his toes for a moment before thinking better of it and dropping back to his heels. "We're too low for the words," he muttered, more to himself than to anyone else. "They're all bending around the ledge."

"We're getting what we need," Anna said.

She kept her eyes on the river, on the way the fire's reflection stretched and broke around the rocks near the bank, then re-formed into longer bands in the deeper channel. The water turned the bonfire into a living thing with two bodies: one standing upright, one lying down, both flickering in time with the wind and the drums.

The rail under her hands vibrated faintly with each shouted "remember." It felt less like a ceremony happening somewhere else, more like a weather system moving through stone and water and the people pressed against it.

Anna didn't say the words with them. She didn't have to. Standing there, with the river breathing at her feet and the tower's hum still ghosting in her muscles, she felt the shape of them all the same.

Whatever else the night decided to bring—the lanterns still being folded at the edges of the crowd, the sky above the canyon darkening toward whatever it had in store—could wait its turn. For now, the scope of her world was simple: fire on stone, light on water, voices marking the fact that they were still here to say anything at all.

The call-and-response thinned, leaving the drums to carry the night.

Closer to the water, other work had begun.

On the ledge just to Anna's right, a group of children and a couple of elders knelt around baskets. Paper rustled. Thin wire clicked softly against itself. Small, careful hands coaxed shapes into being—little frames, square and hexagonal, wrapped in pale paper rubbed with grease so it would take light without burning too quickly.

A boy's lantern tore along a fold. He made a strangled sound of frustration.

"Easy," one of the elders said. She had hair cut close to the scalp and a face folded into dry lines by years of sun and river glare. "It's not a sail, little fish. It only has to hold long enough to be seen."

She pinched the rip flat, smoothed a patch of older paper over it, and handed it back.

Milo drifted toward them, curiosity tugging at his feet the way the river tugged at reeds along the bank. Anna followed at a slower pace, staying just at the edge of the circle.

"You're sending them up?" Milo asked, eyeing the small fuel-cups waiting in a row—tiny pots filled with twisted rag and fat.

"Some up," the elder said. "Some out." She jerked her chin toward the water. "The river gets its share."

A younger girl looked up at Anna, eyes reflecting the bonfire's moving glow from up-canyon. "They say people on the first world used to do this," she said. "Put lights on rivers so lost ones could find their way. Is that true?"

The word first world landed with its usual faint unease. Old-Earth stories were like that—half legend, half leftover briefing notes, none of it quite sitting right.

Anna kept her gaze on the lantern in the girl's hands. The frame was made from shaved-down scrap, sanded smooth where fingers would touch.

"I've heard the story," she said. "Big rivers. Lots of boats. Nights full of floating lights." She let a small shrug carry away the parts she wouldn't promise. "Could be true. Could be the way people here remember it."

The girl considered that, then nodded, apparently satisfied. "Doesn't matter," she decided. "We do it here now."

The elder gave a dry chuckle. "There you have it," she said. "History updated."

Anna huffed the ghost of a laugh. It tugged at the tight places between her ribs but didn't hurt.

The drums shifted again, easing into a slower, more spacious rhythm. Space opened in the noise. The ledge seemed to breathe.

Above the canyon, the last smear of blue in the sky had surrendered to full darkness. Stars pricked through along the narrow strip, faint at first, then sharper as Anna's eyes adjusted. Between them, a deeper glow began to gather—the color of banked coals seen through thin cloth.

The Sky Fire's Remnant.

It flowed. A long, uneven band of red and violet and ghost-pale gold, poured across the seam of visible sky. It seemed motionless until watched for more than a few heartbeats and saw the way one knot of brightness thickened while another thinned, as if someone were stirring it from very far away.

"Whoa," Milo breathed.

Anna said nothing. The sight pinched something deep behind her breastbone, the same way a new storm-reservoir on the horizon sometimes did—too big to take in all at once, too important to look away from.

On the river's surface the nebula doubled itself.

The Ishkar lay almost black where it passed directly under the canyon walls, but out toward the middle the water picked up light: first the scattered orange of the bonfire's reflection, then the steadier glow from hanging lanterns, and then, slowly, the washed-out echo of the Remnant itself. The result was a long, wavering ribbon of color that seemed to run under the ledge as much as past it.

For a moment the world turned itself inside out.

Fire above, fire below. Stone on both sides. A river holding two skies in one body.

Milo's hand found the rail beside Anna's and gripped. She shifted her own so their knuckles just brushed—a touch light enough that he could pretend it was an accident if he wanted.

On the ritual ledge, the caller's voice rose again, thinner now against the wider quiet.

"We remember," they said for a final time.

The answer came back softer. Not everyone joined in. Some just watched the Sky Fire smear itself across the water and kept their mouths closed, as if saying it would make the words too small.

The elder with the close-cropped hair set her hands on her knees and pushed herself upright with a small grunt.

"Alright," she said. "Show the sky where the river runs."

She nodded to the children. One by one, they dipped their lanterns to the cousin with a coal pan, who touched each wick with the same small, precise motion. Flames caught, thin and blue at first, then thickening to a steady yellow.

Some of the lanterns were rigged with tiny balloon skins, hot air already ballooning them out. Others were simple boxes meant for the water. The elder divided them with an easy hand.

"Up," she said, passing a cluster of hot-air ones to the teens. "Out," she told the younger kids, guiding their steps to the edge of the ledge.

A boy with a lantern almost as big as his torso approached the rail near Anna. His eyes flicked from the water to the sky and back.

"If I let go," he said, addressing no one in particular, "how far does it go? All the way to the Sky Fire?"

"Depends," Milo began. "On lift, on—"

Anna tapped the rail with one knuckle, just loud enough to cut him off without making a scene.

"It'll go as far as the air and the river let it," she said. "That's far enough for the sky to see it."

The boy's brow furrowed, parsing that. Then he nodded with solemn satisfaction, as if she'd confirmed something important.

He crouched and set his lantern gently on the water. It rocked once, twice, then the small flame found its balance and the lantern drifted away from the bank, current catching it and folding it into a slow procession of other small lights.

Higher up, the ballooned lanterns began to rise.

They weren't graceful. They bobbed and rolled, bumping knees and hands, bumping each other, before the hot air took full hold. Then they lifted in fits and starts, the canyon's updraft catching them and pulling them toward the narrow strip of sky. Their paper skins glowed from within, small bruises of color against the dark.

"Do you think it helps?" Milo asked quietly, watching one lantern wobble past a jut of rock and correct its climb as the air curled around it.

"Helps what?" Anna said.

He hesitated. "Them. Us. Any of it."

Anna watched a lantern's reflection slide along the Ishkar, stretched to a thin trembling line, then break as it passed over a submerged stone.

"They're still here," she said at last, tilting her head toward the people along the ledge. "Lanterns or no lanterns. But if it makes the night sit easier in their bones? That's a kind of help."

A corner of his mouth kicked up. "Pragmatic optimism," he said, like quoting a half-remembered note.

"Something like that." She let a faint smile show. "Besides, if one of those balloons ever drifts into my flight path, I'll know who to invoice."

That earned a genuine laugh from him, quick and surprised. A couple of nearby kids looked over, grinned, and went back to shepherding their lights.

For a while they simply watched.

Lanterns rose and drifted. Others floated away downriver, small bright seeds sent into the dark. The bonfire's heart sank a little lower. The drums softened, hands easing off the skins, turning the rhythm from declaration to background pulse.

The ache in Anna's shoulders had settled into a deep, even throb. It felt less like injury now and more like the price of something done right.

Footsteps approached along the ledge, light but deliberate.

"Pilot," a voice said at Anna's shoulder.

She turned. Sila stood there, the lines of her face gentler in lanternlight. A faint sheen of damp on her neck and collarbone said she'd been back up to the tower and down again since Anna had last seen her.

"Tower's stable?" Anna asked.

"Filters are behaving," Sila said. "Flow's clear at three taps I checked. I'll pretend not to worry about the rest until morning."

She glanced at the river, at the lanterns working their slow way downstream.

"You two look like you're about to fall where you stand," she added. "We've a hut set aside. It's not fancy, but it doesn't leak. Come."

Anna's first instinct was to protest—something about logs to check, a stabilizer to rethink—but her knees chose that moment to inform her that they had very specific ideas about how much longer they were interested in this whole standing situation.

She gave the river one last look. The Sky Fire's reflection had thinned to a muted smear as the bonfire's light faded, but the steady band overhead still burned.

"Alright," she said. "Lead on."

The path away from the ledge curled back into the cliff, climbing only a little before leveling out. The sounds of the ritual receded with each turn—drums dimming, voices blending into a single low murmur, the river's whisper filling the spaces between.

The guest hut sat on a small shelf of rock set slightly apart from the nearest cluster of dwellings. Its walls were built from dark river stones mortared tight, the roof a curve of salvaged plating anchored by heavy beams. A single glow-lamp hung by the door, its light warm and steady.

Sila lifted the latch and pushed the door open with her shoulder.

Warmth spilled out. Not stifling, just enough to smooth the chill from Anna's skin as she stepped through. The air smelled of old woodsmoke and dried herbs, something sharp and clean threaded through the softer notes—mountain sage, maybe, laid on the rafters to dry.

Inside, the space was simple.

Two cots, low and sturdy, stood against opposite walls, their frames made of planed scrap timber. Wool blankets lay folded at the foot of each, coarse but thick. A small shelf held a clay jug and two cups. In a corner niche, a basin and a short spout promised water without ceremony.

"The cistern's full," Sila said. "Bolt holds. No one will bother you. We'll see about breakfast when the light's back." She met Anna's eyes. "You did what you came to do. Let the canyon mind itself for a few hours."

Anna nodded. "Thanks."

Sila gave a brief, satisfied grunt that served for you're welcome and good night both, then stepped back out. The door shut with a solid, comforting thud. The hut's small noises—wood settling, lamp faintly ticking—rose to fill the gap left by the outside world.

Milo swayed where he stood, the last of the night's alertness draining out of him in a visible wave.

"Boots," Anna said.

He obeyed on reflex more than intent, hands clumsy on the laces. By the time she'd set her own gear bag down by the wall and shrugged out of her jacket, he had managed to get onto his cot. He didn't so much lie down as fold, knees and elbows giving up in stages until gravity arranged him flat.

Anna crossed the hut, pulled the blanket up over him, and tucked the edge in at his shoulders. His breathing had already smoothed into the slow, even pattern she associated with short, hard sleeps between runs.

"Out like a switch," she murmured.

She took the jug down from the shelf, poured herself half a cup, and drank. The water was still faintly warm from the cistern, iron-soft and clean. It sat heavy and good in her stomach.

The wash at the basin was quick but enough—hands, face, the grit at the back of her neck where the harness had rubbed. When she straightened, her reflection wavered in the surface of the remaining water: tired eyes, hair escaping the tie at the nape of her neck, a faint smudge of river dust along one cheek.

"Tomorrow's problem," she told the reflection quietly, and blew out a breath.

She dimmed the lamp to a low ember and crossed to her own cot.

The blanket was rough against her fingertips but warmed fast once she was under it. The cot's slats creaked once in protest, then settled. The hut held the day's last sounds at the edges: a distant, muted thump that might have been a final drumbeat, the soft, steady hush of the Ishkar threaded through stone.

Anna let her muscles release one group at a time—hands, shoulders, jaw—like working through a checklist in reverse. For the first time since the Channel, there was no new variable waiting in the next breath. Filters were seated. Ship was down. Kid was breathing.

The canyon's quiet folded around her, heavy and kind.

She followed it down into sleep.
