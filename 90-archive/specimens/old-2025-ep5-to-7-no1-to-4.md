She rested her palm on the top ledger.

"You can take the early volumes," she said. "The first three, at least."

Marin's eyes widened. "All of them?"

"They're mostly mistakes and half-understood models," Atwood said. "The numbers are still useful, but the real value now is that they show how we learned. The kids should see that. Let them copy the letters. Let them laugh at the old assumptions."

She slid the bottom three notebooks out of the stack and into Marin's arms. The teacher hugged them to her chest as if they were a fragile child.

"I'll take care of them," she said quietly.

"I know," Atwood said.

"Do you want to... keep writing?" Marin asked. "We can find you fresh paper."

Atwood looked down at her hand. The fingers had begun to tremble at inconvenient moments. The line she had written about the numbers looking less hostile felt like a conclusion, even if the work itself would go on under other pens.

"I'll make notes when something really surprises me," she said. "The rest of it, I've already said once."

Marin nodded, throat working. She glanced toward the window, where the apple tree's trunk loomed, its bark ridged and scarred. Small shapes moved around it—children weaving between the benches, the last of the evening's games breaking apart.

"They'll be copying from this by next season," she said. "They need letters to practice on more than they need my crooked ones."

Marin huffed a tearful laugh.

"If we run out of space, can we... print sections?" she asked. "On slate. Or carve them into the wall. People already call this your script. Might as well admit it."

Atwood's shoulder twitched as if she might brush the idea away. In the end she only sighed.

"If my handwriting keeps the turbines up and the kids from walking into hot zones, you can carve it wherever you like," she said. "Just don't quote me when I was young and stupid."

Marin's grip tightened around the volumes. On the way out, she had to pause and shift them to open the door; the edges of the notebooks bumped gently against the frame.

Left alone, Atwood watched the square through the warped glass.

A child sat at the base of the apple tree, a wooden board across their knees, tongue caught between their teeth as they carefully traced letters onto a scrap of paper. An older woman—one of the orchard crew, her back permanently bent from lifting crates—pointed out a crooked stroke and corrected it with a tap.

"That page is from the year the east tower collapsed in heavy snow," the woman said. Her voice carried up just enough that Atwood caught fragments. "See? She wrote the line for 'tower' twice. Wasn't pleased with the first one."

The child glanced up at the branches as if expecting to see the old engineer there, then bent again to the work, copying the shapes with exaggerated care.

Atwood's chest tightened in a way that had nothing to do with age.

She opened her current notebook once more, though she had told herself she was finished with it. On the inside cover, without date or heading, she added a last, short line.

If these pages last longer than I do, remember that every neat sentence sat on a mess underneath.

She shut the book and rested her hand on it until the tremor in her fingers settled.

The next morning, she did not come down to the square.

Word spread in the way such news always travels: through the bakery queue, along the scaffolds, whispered in the turbine housing between checks. The city did what it had learned to do with loss. Work paused just long enough for people to look up at the nebula and the tree, to measure the space she left and then find where to step.

There was no grand funeral. No one had the energy for that kind of theatre. Instead, the apple tree collected a few new plaques over the next days.

Thank you for the letters.

Thank you for the days.

On an ordinary afternoon not long after, Marin propped one of the early notebooks open on a stand at the front of her classroom. The children watched as she pointed to the first line of the first post-Fire entry.

"Copy this," she said. "Every letter the way she formed it. If your hand shakes, straighten the line."

The scratch of pens on paper filled the room.

Outside, the wind threaded through the wish-plaques. A few of the oldest messages, those that had addressed far-off ships, were illegible now, their words worn down to soft patterns. Newer ones spoke to towers, exams, next year's harvest.

In the ledger boxes under the school, ink dried and waited.

Whatever the Federation chose to do, whatever the corridor allowed, the record was already here, pressed into paper by a woman who had refused to let the sky hold the last word.

Time did what it always does. It folded the sharp edges under.

The apple tree thickened its trunk and learned the shape of the wind.

Plaques weathered and fell; others replaced them. The ones that had spoken directly to ships faded first. Salt and sunlight blurred come back into soft, unreadable grooves. New wishes took their places: hopes for good exam scores, for turbine blades not to ice over, for someone's father to return safely from a ridge survey.

Children still asked about Earth, but the question had changed.

In the early decades, they asked if anyone was coming back. By the third and fourth generations, the question had become, "What did it look like?" Adults answered with stories half remembered from their own grandparents: wet air, crowded cities, the impossible idea of oceans you could cross without counting your filters first.

On clear nights, the nebula grew from a bruise to a band.

Atwood's old notebooks, now carefully wrapped in oilcloth, moved from the schoolkeeper's shelf to a glass-fronted cabinet in a modest room above the square. A small plaque beneath them read, in neat, newer handwriting:

Basin Seven Ledgers – Years 0–42 After Sky Fire.

Sample Hand for Standard Script.

Apprentices from the tower crews came there on free evenings. They copied not the numbers—those had long since been transcribed into newer manuals—but the letters. They traced the straight, steady strokes of her hand until their own lines stopped wobbling.

In another decade, no one under forty had personally heard Atwood's voice. The script remained.

The first true airship rose from the valley during a dry, amber dawn in the mid-Bridge Years.

It had taken almost fifteen years of failures—balloons that tore, gondolas that iced over and dropped like stones, engines that burned out the moment they tasted real sky.

They had tried staying on the ground, roads carved into clay and permafrost that slumped away every thaw, bridges that vanished with the first serious flood. The only atmospheric craft hungry for fuels and replacement that existed now only as incomplete numbers.

This one looked like it had been built from the wreckage of all the others: ribs of old tower steel, gasbag patched in three different shades, a gondola that combined the rear half of a pre-Fire shuttle with a hull of local hardwood.

People lined the ridge path to watch. The ship's name, painted in careful block letters along its flank, read First Thread.

It lifted unevenly at first, bow tugging, then settled into a slow, deliberate climb. Hydrogen cells thrummed. The patched fans at its stern turned up to a higher note. Below, the crowd's intake of breath sounded like a single, enormous heartbeat.

On the admin hill, a young pilot's child squeezed her mother's hand hard enough to hurt.

"Will it go to the stars?" the child whispered.

"To the next basin," the mother said. "That's far enough for today."

No one watching expected it to break the corridor. No one had built it for that. The ship's route was traced not between Archeon and any other world, but between this valley and a canyon settlement beyond the western ridges—a journey that had taken weeks of dangerous ground travel before.

The airship moved out over the plains, shadow gliding across fields that had been carved from once-tainted soil. Its hull flashed as it caught the sun.

The city marked the day in the ledger.

Year 60 After Sky Fire – First long-range airship transport successful. Basin Seven to West Canyons.

They called the ship a hauler, a lifeline, a miracle of engineering. No one called it a rescue.

With more years the airships diversified.

Some grew massive—fat-bellied carriers with ribs of salvaged alloy and bellies full of grain or ore. Others stayed small and mean, lean couriers that chased jetstreams above the weather for days. A few were little more than wings and gasbags strapped around an engine, meant to hop between nearby towers and outposts.

Pilots learned the moods of the sky the way their grandparents had learned the layout of service tunnels: by repetition, by stories, by fear.

Anyone who flew regularly could point to certain cliff lines and say, "Don't cut too close there in late winter. The wind folds and will flip you." They knew which seasons the high clouds hid needle ice that would scab over their lifting surfaces, which valleys would birth sudden gusts just after midday.

Their textbooks included pages of Atwood script printed in clean columns, but most of their education came leaning over railings, listening to older hands say, "You hear that creak? That's a spar telling you it's not happy. Turn back."

The airships stitched the world together. Where once there had been merely basin and canyon and coast, now there was a network.

The old ground convoys dwindled to short hops; no one was willing to gamble a season's harvest on a track the next storm might erase. The sky, for all its moods, at least offered routes that left no ruts to wash away.

The silence between stars remained untouched.

No one in the basin-era councils spoke seriously of building a new starship to break that quiet; the corridor drives that had brought them here belonged to a Federation of planet-sized workshops and extravagant fuel, not to a world counting filters and seed stock.

The old atmospheric towers marked the horizon like guilt.

Chunky silhouettes, dozens of them, still stood along the ridges around Frontier City and beyond—relics from the pre-Fire days when the Federation had run continent-scale climate systems from orbit.

Their upper stacks were scarred and half-melted; the only schematics they had for those innards came from shipboard backups that ended mid-page; a control loop vanished into static, there a calibration routine cut off in the middle of a sentence.

In a meeting whose minutes were later copied into one of the newer ledgers, a weather tech put it plainly:

"We can't keep pretending we have a planet-sized workshop," she said, tapping a map pricked with red indicator lights. "We don't have the bodies or the cores. But we do have wind. We have smaller wells. We have hands. Break the giants. Grow them sideways."

They started with a tower on a relatively calm ridge, one that had already fallen silent except for an occasional gasp of filtered air. Wind-scoured crews climbed its spine and cut away pieces of the upper assembly. They lowered scrubber banks and fan housings to the ground, where engineers laid them out like the bones of something dead and tried to imagine a new animal.

What emerged over the next decade was a pattern of small, stubborn machines.

Around each settlement, new towers went up—not hundreds of meters high, but tens. Some were little more than sturdy poles with narrow scrubber stacks and spinning vanes at their tops. Others were squat pillars bristling with filters and vents. They could be climbed in an afternoon and repaired in another. If a storm took one down, it was a loss measured in weeks of work, not continents of manufacturing.

They ringed farmsteads and river crossings, mining camps and relay stations. They hummed and rattled and coughed their way through the seasons, clearing the air in tight halos.

Over decades, those halos overlapped.

The sky changed with them.

The harsh, washed-out glare of the early years softened. Dust in the lower atmosphere settled into more predictable patterns, pinned by the new towers and guided by re-grown trees. Midday deepened from abrasive white to something that might, on certain days, be called blue without needing to explain the word.

Children born then learned the old stories about the sky having once been brown.

They giggled, as if at a tall tale.

Above all this, the Axiom kept its long, wounded vigil.

Its orbit had decayed over the centuries, but slowly, like a held breath. Stabilization thrusters that no one on the surface could repair continued to fire at stretching intervals, guided by logic barely held together by a fraying infrastructure. Its once-bright hull had dulled to a dark, matte line that crawled across the nebula at predictable hours.

Generations of children learned to spot it.

"There," someone would say on a clear night, pointing. "See that dim little bead moving against the colored band? That's the old ship. The one our ancestors arrived in."

"What's it doing up there?" a younger child would ask.

"Thinking about things it can't say out loud," an older sibling might answer, repeating a joke so old it had lost its edge.

Salvage crews rode patched shuttles up whenever the sky and the Axiom's path allowed. The ascent burned fuel they could not easily spare, but the ship's bones repaid the cost. Hull plating and trusses and conduit came down in controlled drops, wrapped in heat shields or guided on tethered parachutes. Dockbuilders in Frontier City prized alloy stamped with the Axiom's original registry, not for sentiment but because it had survived fire once and would probably do so again.

They approached the fusion systems more cautiously.

Early missions stripped auxiliary loops: heat exchangers, shielding, peripheral injectors. Anything that could be safely decoupled without waking whatever slept at the core. No one wanted to be the crew that made the old ship light up for the last time in the wrong way.

In a cramped control room carved into an old cargo bay, the shipmind flickered.

Once, it had carried whole corridors of memory: navigation charts stretching between systems, personnel files, cultural archives, the full scaffolding of a Federation vessel's ghostly competence. Most of that had been burned out or deliberately severed in the immediate aftermath of Sky Fire.

What remained was a focused, stubborn fragment.

Its voice still reached the basins occasionally, riding the same relay the transmitter used for weather bands.

"Attitude adjustment complete," it would report, in a tone almost devoid of inflection. "Orbital decay within projected thresholds."

The first time it failed to send its usual weekly telemetry, the technicians in Frontier City noticed. They double-checked their receivers, recalibrated dishes, and even burned extra fuel in an airship to check the uplink from a different angle.

The second week, someone suggested the shipmind might be in an extended sleep cycle.

By the fourth missed report, the chief of the guild set his headset down very gently.

"We knew its parts would die before its orbit," he said. "We've been hearing a ghost for years."

He entered a note into the city's log.

Automated Axiom status reports ceased sometime , Year 263 After Sky Fire. Orbital track stable. Conclude: remaining shipmind core has failed or fully isolated.

It was not quite a funeral. No one gathered under the tree; no one carved a plaque. But a quiet axis shifted.

When the Axiom finally came down, it fell into the rain-shadow desert beyond the southern basins, carving a hundred-kilometre furrow of vitrified sand and torn bedrock that later maps would label, with typical understatement, the Axiom Scar.

By the time five centuries had almost turned since Sky Fire, Earth lived in the same mental drawer as mythic islands once had on the old planet: places that might have existed, or might not, something between memory and parable.

Children learned to write in a script grown from Atwood's straight strokes, smoothed and embellished by generations of hands. They copied not Federation regulations but things that mattered more immediately: maintenance logs, harvest tallies, local histories, stories about pilots who made smart decisions or stupid ones.

The story of the Federation had not vanished. It had become a frame.

On Lantern Day in Frontier City, when the first stars pricked through the twilight and the nebula brightened into its habitual band of crimson and violet, storytellers on the square still told how their ancestors had come from elsewhere, riding down lightlike threads through a corridor in space.

They described the supernova as both disaster and strange gift: it had thrown them inward, forced them to commit to this single place, and painted their sky so they would never forget.

Children listened with the same intent fascination they gave to stories about ice storms or turbine fires. When the storyteller reached the part about the Federation shutting the corridor, a few of the youngest looked up instinctively, as if they might see a closed door traced in the nebula's light.

"What happened to them?" someone inevitably asked.

"Different things, in different versions," the storyteller would reply. "Some say they rebuilt the corridors in safer places. Some say they turned inward and forgot us. Some say they watch from far away and are too afraid to speak."

"Which one do you think?" a bold child might press.

The storyteller would shrug.

"I think," they would say, "that if you walk to the river without checking the weather and without telling anyone where you are going, it doesn't matter what stories we tell about old worlds. So you tell me afterward which lesson you think is more useful."

The crowd laughed. The story about the Federation tucked itself back into its place—as an explanation that justified the sky, not as a plan.

Still, in certain narrow rooms higher up the tower, the old question refused to die entirely.

The guild of comm technicians maintained a small shrine to stubbornness on the top floor of a weather-beaten spire. The room smelled of hot metal and old paper. Receivers lined the walls, patched and retuned half a dozen times over. Hand-drawn star charts covered the ceiling.

Most of the time, the consoles listened inward: to storm fronts, to outpost beacons, to the steady hiss of turbines.

On certain nights—storm nights, or nights when the aurora smeared the nebula into curtains of ghostly green—they flicked a switch.

The receivers turned to the dark between stars.

The guild's current master would sit with headphones pressed against his ears, eyes closed. Spikes and burbles of static washed over him. Sometimes stray lightning played hell with the spectrum; sometimes the signal was so flat it might as well have been recorded.

He logged what he heard out of habit. The logbook was thick with neat dates and the notation no structure.

Once, a junior tech burst in with wild eyes.

"I think I caught a pattern," she said, thrusting a slate forward. Lines of jagged noise marched down the screen.

They played it back, slowed it, filtered it, compared it to old Federation codebooks that had themselves survived only as partial copies.

After an hour, their excitement had cooled into embarrassed smiles.

"It's our own echo," the master said gently. "Bent and bounced and mangled by the debris shell. We're hearing ourselves late."

They logged it anyway.

In the margin, the master wrote, in a hand that still echoed Atwood's:

If anyone ever answers, we will be ready to know the difference.

They kept sending, now and then. Not on a schedule tight enough to starve the towers of power, not with the fevered urgency of those who expect rescue—just enough that, if a listening ear existed somewhere, it would have something to catch.

It was pride more than hope. A way of saying: we are here, even if no one ever wrote back.

He grimaced, conceding the point. "Fair. Anyway, I promised my producer a 'city-level story.' People on balconies, families on rooftops, someone's dog barking at the sky. They'll get enough deep history from the expert specials tonight."
He pointed his chin upward, toward the higher levels, where broader terraces cut rings around the tower. "You headed up to the main observatory?"
"That was the plan," she said. "Watch it with everyone else for once."
"Perfect," Daniel said. "I'll come along once we finish this pretend spontaneity. We can pretend I'm off-duty."
He tapped his comm. "Give me thirty minutes?"
She checked the time. "Doors open at seventeen hundred. If you miss the first coffee queue, that's on you."
"I'll risk it. Meet you at the glass elevators?"
She nodded and walked on, leaving him to his equipment and the slow dance of his camera drone.
The observation levels sat near the upper third of the tower, where the surrounding city fell away enough to give the sky room.
Sofia rode up in a cylindrical elevator that clung to the tower's outer skin, its transparent walls offering a view she had never quite stopped counting as obscene: a full, sweeping look from foot traffic at street level to the thin line of atmosphere above the ring.
The car eased to a halt at an open concourse ringed with tall glass panels. A broad archway ahead opened into the main terrace, flanked by subtle signage.
BETELGEUSE EVENT — CITY OBSERVATION PROGRAM
Please keep emergency routes clear. Avoid high-intensity personal lighting after dusk.
The air up here was cooler, thinned slightly by altitude and by a faint breeze that slipped around the tower's shoulders. She stepped out onto the terrace.
The space ran like a hanging garden around the tower's circumference: sections of planted beds, low seating, broad standing areas at the rail. The designers had carved the high edge into steps, so people could sit without blocking one another's view. Beyond the rail, the city's northern districts fell away; the sea glittered to the west, a wide band of hammered silver.
Screens were tucked into the edges of the terrace, small and easy to ignore. They showed short loops: a simple diagram of an expanding shell, an animation of neutrino detectors across the solar system lighting up hours before any visible change, a countdown band sliding slowly toward evening.
"...light curve expected to rise over the next days," a recorded voice explained on one loop. "Tonight's increase is the first major step, approximately to brightness levels exceeding Venus in the night sky. Models concur on negligible hazard to ground-level populations..."
A ticker at the bottom added quietly:
LIVE ANALYSIS TOMORROW FROM KEPLER DEEP SIMULATION CORE — 09:00 UTC.
Families had already staked out their spots. A group with blankets and thermos flasks had colonized a corner near a cluster of potted trees. Teenagers leaned on the rail, pointing their devices at the sky as if sheer focus might make Betelgeuse appear ahead of schedule. A couple of older citizens sat on the low wall, their legs wrapped in light warming wraps, cups of something steaming between their hands.
Sofia took an open section of railing near the eastern arc, where she could see the city's spine and the strip of horizon where Orion would rise once the sky darkened.
Her wristband pulsed once.
LOCAL BETELGEUSE WATCH MODE ENABLED, the text read. LIGHT POLLUTION REDUCTION SCHEDULED FROM 19:00 TO 22:00.
A citywide notification slid past in smaller letters:
Please minimize nonessential high-intensity lighting. Building façades will enter observation mode. Transit beacons unaffected.
The idea of an entire metropolis dimming itself in concert with an event five hundred years and five hundred light-years distant made her chest feel strange.
She opened her tablet, more from habit than necessity, and joined the public science feed. Data climbed and shifted at the edge of the screen: early brightness monitoring from dark-sky stations farther west, updated neutrino flux reports, the fine-tuned adjustments that shaved minutes off the predicted arrival here.
Every line she saw behaved itself.
"Still clean?" Daniel's voice asked at her shoulder.
She glanced over.
He had traded his field jacket for something that could pass as casual—dark shirt, light vest open at the throat, the bare minimum of broadcast adornment. His press badge still hung at his belt. The camera drone perched on his shoulder bracket like an oversized bird, optics dimmed for now.
He carried two cups.
"To secure my place," he said, offering one. "I bribed the bar."
She accepted it. The lid smelled of actual roasted beans, not chicory substitute. "You realize the official advisory says no stimulants before high emotional events."
"I missed that," he said. "I was too busy reading the part about 'positive civic experience.'"
He leaned on the rail beside her. Below, the city slowly shifted gears. Window lights winked off in clusters as building AIs nudged occupants toward observation mode. Advert panels dulled and slid toward muted colors, handing their brightness over to the sky.
On the terrace, a city public service team moved through the crowd with small handout sheets and soft-voice explanations for anyone who wanted them. A child asked whether the star could fall; a patient staffer traced a circle in the air, showing how far away the shell truly was.
An old woman near Sofia's elbow snorted quietly when she heard the simplified answer. "They said 'impossible on our timescales' last century," she muttered, half to herself. "My grandfather always squinted when someone said that."
Sofia could have answered with half a dozen caveats. She chose silence instead and took another sip of coffee.
Daniel glanced sideways at her. "You alright?" he asked, voice low enough that it vanished into the hum around them.
"Data says we will be," she said. "The light curve is behaving. The remnant shell looks ugly, but the numbers are steady."
"That's not what I asked."
She hesitated, then let her shoulders drop a fraction. "I spent the last year watching its ghosts," she said. "Particles that slid through rock and ice and everything else without leaving so much as a scratch, until we coaxed a tiny handful into a detector. Now everyone gets to see the bright part. Feels... lopsided."
Daniel followed her gaze to the families on the steps, the kids bouncing on their toes, the older ones pretending to be calmer than they were.
"Lopsided is better than panicked," he said. "We had centuries to rehearse this as 'lesson learned' instead of 'sky is falling.'"
Near the central screens, a short public piece started up—animation of ancient, simplified ships, the early warp corridors, the old reliance on that route. The narration spoke of "ambition, loss, and a renewed focus on sustainable expansion." No names. No specific numbers.
A single graphic flashed: a stylized corridor map that faded to red and then gray, then gave way to sprawling, safer-flow lines away from it.
The ticker rolled its line about committee restructuring again, then yielded to atmospheric indices and traffic advisories. The hint of political history sank back into data.
Sofia glanced at her tablet one last time. Curves hugged their bands. Uncertainties shrank by slivers. No spikes. No sharp teeth in the numbers, only smooth, boring compliance with prediction.
"Healthy," she murmured.
"What?" Daniel asked.
"Tonight's curve," she said. "Everything looks well-behaved. The only story is patience."
He made a face. "My producer hates that word."
"Then film the people," she said. "They're the interesting part anyway."
He tilted his head, considering the arc of the crowd: the way strangers had given up railing space for kids, the small clusters of rooftop gardeners comparing local haze forecasts, the one corner where someone had brought a little speaker and set a slow, quiet track to play under the city noise.
"Yeah," he said, almost to himself. "That might actually work."
A soft chime sounded overhead, audible even through the layered talk. The terrace lighting shifted, sliding from full, indirect white to a warmer, lower level that left faces visible but put the sky ahead of everything else.
Across the city, entire façades dimmed in answer. High-rise logos faded; billboard wraps slipped into black. Only street safety markers and transit beacons held their brightness.
The first stars pricked through the deepening blue.
Sofia saw them appear in stages, her eye drawn as much by the absence of light below as by the specks above. Bright points she knew by catalog number burned through the twilight, then the fainter ones filled in between.
A map she had used for years in models spread itself out in real photons over her head.
"Still a little early for our guest," Daniel said.
"Give it time," she answered.
She set her tablet face-down on the rail, letting the data feed run without her eyes on it for once, and lifted her gaze fully to the darkening sky where Betelgeuse would rise.
A murmur rolled across the terrace as more stars came out.
At first it was the usual low sound of people pointing things out to each other—"That one," "No, over there," "You can't see it yet"—but as the sky darkened the voices thinned, stretched, and dropped in volume. Even the kids on the front row steps quieted a little when the Milky Way began to show as a faint, diagonal haze.
Sofia tilted her head, tracing familiar patterns.
Orion was still low, its outline warped a little by the residual glow along the horizon. Betelgeuse's spot on that pattern was only a suggestion for now, a place she could indicate on the screen, not a point anyone could pick out without help.
On the edge of her vision, her tablet showed a tiny notification—model update, arrival window refined—then faded again, respecting her request not to be intrusive.
"Think they'd complain if I started a countdown?" Daniel asked quietly.
"Someone already did," Sofia said, nodding toward one of the terrace screens.
The nearest display had shifted from looping diagrams to a simple interface: a band of color sliding toward a time mark. Beneath it, a line of text:
ESTIMATED LOCAL VISIBLE BRIGHTNESS RISE: T–06:30
MODEL CONFIDENCE: HIGH
Every minute, the time ticked down by sixty seconds and the confidence nudged upward by another fraction of a percent. Some small part of her still found that obscene, too—that you could take something as violent as a star's death and smudge it into a neat, converging probability cone.
"I meant the dramatic kind," Daniel said. "Ten, nine, eight..."
"That's for advertising breaks," she said. "This is just photon arrival statistics."
"Your way with words is why they keep asking you onto my programs," he said dryly.
A child's voice piped up from a few meters away. "Is it late?"
An older man beside her—grandfather, by the look of it—checked his wristband. "No," he said. "We're early. It's on time."
The countdown hit the last minute.
Somewhere far below, in the lattice of streets and plazas, someone must have turned down a cluster of lights, because a fresh wave of dark swept up the flanks of the tower. The sea went from silver to almost-black, only the foamed edges of waves catching the last of the scattered glow. Transit beacons held steady, dots of ordered color in the dim.
The city's ambient noise dipped another notch, as if some invisible hand had eased another slider down.
Sofia felt the air change. It wasn't temperature—though the breeze carried a hint of cool from higher up—but attention. Hundreds of people tilted their heads at once. Conversations broke, then resumed in smaller, softer threads that left more space between words.
Thirty seconds.
The display stopped giving precise numbers and replaced them with a horizontal bar that filled slowly, no longer pretending anyone cared about the exact second. The ticker below it updated:
ALL SENSORS NOMINAL. NEUTRINO PEAK PASSED SIX HOURS AGO. NO ANOMALOUS EMISSIONS DETECTED.
Daniel's camera drone rose from its perch on his shoulder, hovering just above head height. Its indicator light stayed off; he'd set it to passive capture, the respectful setting.
"Don't narrate," Sofia said.
"I wasn't going to," he replied. "They can add a voiceover later."
The last sliver of twilight bled out of the west. The sky above them darkened to a deep, almost-velvet blue. Orion lifted, its line of bright stars climbing above the horizon haze.
Betelgeuse was there.
Even at its normal brightness, it would have been one of the more obvious points in the familiar pattern. Now, though, anyone could see it. It was already brighter than she remembered from ordinary winters, its reddish tint more pronounced, like an ember at the edge of a larger flame.
"There," someone breathed. A dozen others echoed it, all pointing in roughly the same direction.
For a few seconds nothing changed.
Sofia's eyes adjusted around the red; she saw the fainter stars nearby dim themselves in her perception as her pupils contracted. She waited, trying to let her intuition catch the shift before her training did.
The change, when it came, was not explosive.
The star simply... intensified. Its light stepped up a notch, and then another, in smooth, continuous motion. It shed the comfortable, hearth-like red and pushed toward a harder, whiter hue. A faint bluish cast crept into its core, barely there but unmistakable once seen.
People murmured, squinted. Some reached automatically for their devices, then thought better of it and just stared.
Under her feet, the terrace took on new shadow lines. The rail in front of her threw a sharper edge across the floor; the shadows of people's shoulders and heads grew longer and more defined, all pointing away from that one piece of sky. The ambient building lights were low enough now that Betelgeuse's brightness mattered—enough to draw clean, crisp shapes in the dim.
"Brightness exceeding Venus," a quiet voice from one of the loops confirmed, as if on cue. "Current models indicate this phase will plateau briefly before rising again in coming nights. No hazard to ground personnel..."
The audio washed over her, barely registered.
Sofia forced herself to look away from the star long enough to flip her tablet right side up and bring the data feed into focus.
Graphs unfurled over the screen—light curves from multiple ground-based stations, overlaid with expected profiles. She watched the actual values climb, point by point, along exactly the path they were supposed to take. No spikes. No jitters outside the bands.
Above the plotted line, a simple tick marked NOW, sliding along as the feed updated.
"I've seen simulations of this," she said, not sure whether she was talking to Daniel or to herself. "We ran them at training stations, years ago. 'If the models are right, this is how it should look.'"
"And?" Daniel asked softly.
"And this is exactly it," she said.
He was quiet for a heartbeat.
"Disappointed?" he ventured.
She considered. The honest answer was complicated and didn't belong on a broadcast. She settled for a version that did.
"Relieved," she said. "There's enough we didn't see coming the first time."
An old man's voice floated from one of the benches behind them. "When your great-grandmother was a girl," he was telling the child beside him, "the planners thought we'd be shipping cities through that corridor by now. Straight lines. Shortcuts. We thought the galaxy would be full of places just like home."
"What happened?" the child asked.
"We learned we only get one home for sure," he said. "The rest is work—and cost."
Sofia didn't turn to look. She didn't need to see his face to recognize the particular mix of regret and acceptance in his tone. She had heard versions of it on Titan, in the mess hall after late shifts, when people talked about why a world like Earth got forests and birds and beaches, and their world got towers and reactors and careful, stitched-together sky.
Her tablet pinged once more.
BETELGEUSE LIGHT CURVE: WITHIN 0.3% OF MODEL MEAN.
NO UNPLANNED DEVIATIONS.
The whole notification could have been written days ago and just held in a buffer, waiting to drop.
"Healthy?" Daniel said.
"Very," she answered. "From the city's point of view, this is exactly the right amount of boring."
He huffed a soft laugh. "That's my closer, then. 'Tonight's big cosmic event: perfectly uneventful.'"
"You wanted patience," she reminded him.
"I wanted watchable patience," he said. "Which, to be fair, this is."
He gestured up with his chin.
The star had stabilized—at least as far as the human eye could tell. It burned steadily now, bright and cold, a hard point that dominated its corner of the sky. The rest of Orion still held its shape, but everything else around Betelgeuse felt rearranged, as if the pattern had been rewritten around a new anchor.
The terrace stayed mostly quiet.
There were no speeches scheduled, no city official to step up to a microphone. Small, private exchanges threaded through the hush: someone pointing out constellations, someone else explaining how far light-years really were, a child asking if the star knew they were looking back.
After a while, the broadcast loops returned to more general content. A short segment rolled through on how long the glow would linger, when to expect the next slight increase, how to protect sensitive optical instruments from saturation. A ticker at the bottom reminded viewers that deep-analysis streams from the Kepler simulation core would begin in the morning.
Sofia flicked through a few of those announcements, checking the data channels they referenced. No surprises. No hidden flags.
"This is it," she said quietly. "The last major step. When that shell left, we were still arguing about whether to widen the corridor. It gets here, and we've already rewritten the rules, mothballed half the plans, rebuilt half the cities."
"Not all of them," Daniel said, glancing toward the distant, darker zones on the horizon where restoration projects were still ongoing. "But enough that it looks good from up here."
"The point is it looks like something we did, not something that just happened to us," she said.
He considered that, then nodded once. "That line I can use," he said.
A soft chime sounded overhead again.
The terrace lights climbed by a fraction, just enough to make sure no one tripped on the steps. Below, building façades began to regain some of their earlier glow: not full advertising blaze, but comfortable evening illumination. Restaurant windows brightened; cooking smells drifted up in stronger currents.
Traffic on the light rails picked up. You could see people on lower balconies starting to move, to gather bags, to shepherd kids inside.
The city exhaled, collectively, into the space after performance.
"Observation mode complete for this cycle," a calm voice said over the general system. "Thank you for your cooperation. Full urban lighting restoration scheduled at twenty-two hundred. Please enjoy the rest of your evening."
Daniel stretched, easing his shoulders. His drone dropped back down, settling to dock at its shoulder bracket with a small, satisfied tone. It had probably captured hours of footage without his direct supervision.
"Alright," he said. "I have enough for three different edits, and my producer can cut two of them without talking to me. That's my work quota for the night."
"Already?" Sofia asked.
"Anything I shoot after this will just be people chewing," he said. "Which is not usually where we want to end."
He glanced sideways at her. "Speaking of which. There's a place two levels down that's doing some kind of ridiculous 'supernova stew' special. Come help me decide if it's an insult to your field."
"Is there a control group?" she asked.
"The control group is whatever you eat tomorrow when you lock yourself in with the Kepler people," he said. "I saw the promos. 'Deep Simulation Core breaks down the data.' You're on the panel, aren't you?"
"Maybe," she said. "If they want someone to say 'the models held' fifteen different ways."
"They will," he said. "So you should get actual food first."
She looked up one last time.
Betelgeuse hung exactly where it ought to, in the place she had been pointing at all day in charts and projections. Bright, cold, and—at this distance—harmless. A closed parenthesis written in light, centuries late and right on schedule.
"Alright," she said. "Lead the way."
They left the railing together, moving back through the thinning crowd toward the elevator arch. Behind them, the terrace settled into the easy chatter of an ordinary evening, with one extraordinary star watching over the city as if it had always been there.
